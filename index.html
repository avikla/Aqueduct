Brief explanation

This Roman-era water system, constructed between mid 2nd century BC to the 1st century AC, relied solely on gravity. At its heart are three large reservoirs known as Solomon’s Pools, located southwest of Bethlehem. The total length is 82.5 kilometers.

 

These pools were fed from the south by two main channels, each sourcing water from several wells (marked in red):

  • The Arrub line (green 41,308m) - It begins at the Arrub pool and includes three tunnels.
  • The Biyar line (purple 5,012m) - It starts at Ein-Biyar with a long tunnel before passing through the Dahr-Baku tunnel.

 

From Solomon’s Pools, two aqueducts carried water to Jerusalem:

  • The lower aqueduct (red 21,447m). A longer route passes through two tunnels before reaching the Temple Mount. It remained in use until roughly 100 years ago.
  • The upper aqueduct (blue 14,735m). Positioned about 30 meters higher than the lower aqueduct. 
    West of Bethlehem, it employed an inverted-siphon made of stone. 
    Its exact path into northern Jerusalem remains uncertain, so two possible routes are shown in green and orange with bridge or inverted-siphon options. 

 

Tunnels are indicated in yellow, along with their known construction shafts (red circles). 

Archaeological discoveries are noted with green diamonds and are clickable. Many other points on the map are clickable, providing additional information and links to relevant articles.

 

Bonus: The system of Gihon-Siloah (Silowan) that was built in the 6th century BC is added to the map south of the Temple Mountain.
